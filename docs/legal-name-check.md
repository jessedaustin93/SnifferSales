## Risk Notes

`SnifferOps` should be treated as an internal/software codename, not the final
public product brand.

The main concern is that `sniffer` is a common term in network/security/RF
monitoring contexts. A descriptive or crowded root term can make a mark harder
to register, harder to enforce, or easier for another owner to challenge.

The branding concern is just as important: `SnifferOps` is easy to confuse with
other `Sniffer`, `SensorOps`, `SpecterOps`, and field-ops style names. It also
sounds more like a product category or internal operations tool than a simple,
distinctive consumer-facing name.

Related search examples found during the first pass:

- USPTO's own search guidance says likelihood of confusion can block a mark
  when a similar mark is used with related goods or services:
  <https://www.uspto.gov/trademarks/search/likelihood-confusion>
- USPTO's trademark search system is the official starting point:
  <https://tmsearch.uspto.gov/>
- USPTO's trademark search help page:
  <https://www.uspto.gov/trademarks/search>
- A third-party historical writeup says `SNIFFER` has been used as a network
  traffic analysis mark tied to Network General / NetScout:
  <https://dpiconsortium.org/resources/sniffer/>
- `Sniffer Field Ops` exists as a software/app name in a different field
  service context:
  <https://www.snifferrobotics.com/sniffer-field-ops>
- `SpecterOps` is an active cybersecurity-adjacent mark and company name. It is
  not an exact match, but it is worth checking because the suffix and field are
  close enough to include in clearance review:
  <https://trademarks.justia.com/876/73/specterops-87673545.html>

## Public Name Criteria

A stronger public name should:

- Be short, simple, and easy to remember.
- Be easy to say and spell.
- Be distinctive enough that people do not mishear it, misname it, or confuse it
  with adjacent security/network tools.
- Have clean domain and handle options.
- Work across software-only, Pi appliance, SDR, portable, and lab/station SKUs.
- Suggest situational awareness, signals, perimeter, field use, or passive
  sensing without sounding generic.
- Avoid generic network/security terms like `sniffer`, `packet`, `scanner`, and
  `monitor` as the dominant source-identifying word.
- Avoid implying offensive surveillance or unauthorized interception.

The rough target is a name with the clarity and memorability of a broad consumer
brand: immediate, pronounceable, hard to confuse, and not locked to one hardware
configuration.
